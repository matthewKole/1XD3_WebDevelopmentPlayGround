Old Man Yells at Cloud

An incident at the Springfield Park today left many onlookers surprised. A local resident, known for his grumpiness and cynicism, was seen shouting at the clouds. The man in question is Abraham Simpson, a resident of the Springfield Retirement Castle. Eyewitnesses reported that Abraham was walking through the park when he suddenly stopped and looked up at the sky. Without any apparent reason, he started yelling at the clouds, attracting the attention of those around him. The incident has sparked interest among the residents of the Springfield Retirement Castle, many of whom have known Abraham for years. We talked to some of them to get their thoughts on this peculiar event. "Oh, that's just Abe being Abe," said fellow resident Jasper Beardly. "He's always had a bit of a temper and isn't afraid to voice his opinions, even if it's just to the clouds." "I don't know what to make of it," said Hans Moleman. "But then again, I've never been one to judge others for their quirks." "I think it's just a sign of his age," said another resident, Granny Simpson. "At our age, you can afford to do things like yell at the clouds and not worry about what others think." While some may see Abraham's behavior as quirky or eccentric, the residents of the Springfield Retirement Castle seem to view it as a harmless expression of his personality.

Polar Bears are in Better Physical Shape than 25 Years Ago, Despite Sea Ice Losses

Polar bears are in better physical health than they were 25 years ago, despite the decrease in sea ice, reveals new research.

The well-being of the iconic white mammals living around the Norwegian island of Svalbard has improved in the face of climate change, likely due to better opportunities to hunt. The scientists were ‘surprised’ that the bears’ fat reserves have increased since the year 2000, even while sea ice levels decreased.

Farmers Enjoy Record Spring Harvests Despite Drought Thanks to Mixture of New and Old Methods

Farming adaptations have seen Canada’s farmers turn out record harvests in the middle of a 5-year drought.

Truly unsavory conditions, like oppressive rainfall followed by an immediate return to drought, would typically have left the wheat on Simon Ellis’ fields shriveled and worthless. Instead, plump grains were ready to be scooped up by his combine. He contributed the extra grain—almost all of which will be exported abroad to developing countries—to a national harvest total that tops any on record despite a drought that started in 2020.
